\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Standard human capital theory treats the depreciation rate $\delta_H$ as a biological constant. This paper shows it is endogenous to the rate of AI capability advancement: $\delta_H^o(t) = \delta_0 + \lambda_o \cdot \dot{\Omega}_{AI}^o(t)$, where $\dot{\Omega}_{AI}^o$ is the rate of AI improvement in tasks relevant to occupation $o$, measured using AI benchmark time series (MMLU, HumanEval, MATH) mapped to occupations via O*NET knowledge areas. I embed this endogenous depreciation in a Ben-Porath lifecycle model where workers choose between investing in routine-cognitive ($H^C$) or augmentable-cognitive ($H^A$) capital. I prove five propositions: (1)~a switching age $a^*$ exists beyond which all investment shifts to $H^A$; (2)~$\partial a^*/\partial \dot{\Omega} < 0$---faster AI advancement causes earlier switching; (3)~a velocity threshold $\dot{\Omega}^{**}$ exists above which $H^C$ investment has negative NPV at any age; (4)~the skill half-life $t_{1/2}^o = \ln 2 / (\delta_0 + \lambda_o \cdot \dot{\Omega}_o)$ varies from 2 to 40+ years across occupations; and (5)~welfare losses from using constant-$\delta$ models are increasing in $\dot{\Omega}$. Empirically, using Colombian household survey data ($N = 71{,}167$), I find that the depreciation effect is \textit{asymmetric by formality}: AI advancement depreciates experience in the informal sector ($\gamma_4 = -0.0013$, $p < 0.001$) but appreciates it in the formal sector ($\gamma_4 = +0.0009$, $p < 0.001$). Workers aged 46--65 face the strongest depreciation ($\gamma_4 = -0.0039$, $p < 0.001$). These findings imply that educational investment horizons should shorten for routine-cognitive skills and that reskilling policy must account for the formal/informal divide.\\[6pt]
\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} J24, J31, O33, I26\\[3pt]
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} human capital depreciation, skill obsolescence, artificial intelligence, skill half-life, lifecycle model, developing economies
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

The Mincerian earnings function assumes that the returns to experience follow a stable, concave path determined by biological depreciation of skills---approximately 1--3\% per year \citep{mincer1974schooling, heckman2006earnings}. This assumption has been foundational for fifty years of labor economics \citep{degrip2002obsolescence, weber2014depreciation}. Yet in an era where AI benchmark scores improve by 20--60\% annually---with HumanEval code generation rising from 29\% to 94\% in four years, and MATH competition scores from 34\% to 97\% in three---the assumption that skill depreciation is constant and biological strains credibility.

This paper proposes that the depreciation rate of cognitive human capital is \textbf{endogenous to the rate of AI capability advancement}. When AI systems rapidly improve in tasks relevant to an occupation, the economic value of experience accumulated in those tasks declines faster than biological aging would predict. The formal object introduced is the \textit{skill half-life}---a concept originating with \citet{machlup1962production}---the time for a cognitive skill's economic value to reach 50\% of its initial level, which I show varies from under 3 years (for programming and routine data analysis) to over 40 years (for physical therapy and surgery) depending on how fast AI is advancing in the relevant task domain.

The paper makes four contributions. First, I develop a dynamic human capital model extending \citet{benporath1967production} where workers choose between investing in routine-cognitive capital ($H^C$, which AI substitutes for) and augmentable-cognitive capital ($H^A$, which AI amplifies), under a depreciation regime where $\delta_{H^C}$ is increasing in the rate of AI advancement $\dot{\Omega}_{AI}$ while $\delta_{H^A}$ is decreasing. I prove five propositions characterizing the optimal investment path, the existence of a switching age, and the conditions under which routine-cognitive investment has negative net present value.

Second, I construct the first empirical measure of occupation-specific AI advancement rates $\dot{\Omega}_o$ by mapping AI benchmark time series (MMLU, HumanEval, MATH, GSM8K, ARC) to occupations through O*NET knowledge areas, following the cognitive ability intermediation approach of \citet{tolan2021measuring}.

Third, I estimate the endogenous depreciation model using Colombian household survey data ($N = 71{,}167$) and discover a striking asymmetry: AI advancement \textit{depreciates} experience in the informal sector ($\gamma_4 = -0.0013$, $p < 0.001$) but \textit{appreciates} it in the formal sector ($\gamma_4 = +0.0009$, $p < 0.001$). This is consistent with the augmentation channel identified in \citet{espinal2026ahc}: formal-sector workers with experience can leverage AI tools to amplify their accumulated expertise, while informal-sector workers face pure skill obsolescence.

Fourth, I derive policy implications for educational investment horizons, reskilling program design, and curriculum composition---all of which depend critically on the occupation-specific skill half-life.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

This paper sits at the intersection of four literatures: human capital depreciation, lifecycle models, AI capability measurement, and AI--labor market interactions.

\subsection{Human Capital Depreciation}

The standard treatment of depreciation in the Mincerian framework assumes a constant rate, typically estimated at 1--3\% per year from the concavity of age-earnings profiles \citep{mincer1974schooling, heckman2006earnings}. \citet{dinerstein2022depreciation} provide the cleanest causal estimate---4.3\% per year---using quasi-random teacher assignment in Greece. \citet{degrip2002obsolescence} distinguish \textit{technical obsolescence} (biological wear) from \textit{economic obsolescence} (technology-driven value decline), finding that one-third of tertiary-education skills become obsolete within seven years. \citet{weber2014depreciation} estimates that higher education depreciates \textit{faster} than lower education, and that depreciation is highest for routine cognitive and non-routine interactive tasks. \citet{neuman1995vintage} disentangle vintage from age effects, finding faster depreciation for newer educational vintages. \citet{gathmann2010general} show that task-specific human capital accounts for up to 52\% of wage growth, and that workers move to occupations with similar task requirements---implying that depreciation in one task domain need not destroy all accumulated capital.

None of these papers makes depreciation endogenous to the rate of technological change. The present paper closes this gap by linking $\delta_H$ to AI benchmark improvement rates.

\subsection{Lifecycle Models}

\citet{benporath1967production} establishes the optimal control framework: workers invest in human capital when young (lower opportunity cost, longer horizon), generating concave age-earnings profiles. \citet{heckman1976lifecycle} embeds this in a labor supply model, and \citet{cunha2007technology} extends it to multi-dimensional skill formation with self-productivity and dynamic complementarity. \citet{chari1991vintage} introduces vintage-specific skills in a growth model: each technology requires its own skill set, and faster technological change shifts the skill distribution toward more recent vintages. My model adapts the Chari-Hopenhayn insight to the AI context: generative AI creates a new ``vintage'' of cognitive tasks, and the depreciation of older vintages ($H^C$) is endogenous to how quickly this new vintage advances.

\subsection{AI Capability Measurement}

\citet{tolan2021measuring} provide the key methodological bridge: they map 328 AI benchmarks to 14 cognitive abilities and 59 generic tasks, enabling the connection between AI capability curves and occupational impact. The Epoch AI Capabilities Index (ECI) \citep{epochai2026} aggregates multiple benchmarks into a single general capability measure using Item Response Theory. I build on this approach but construct \textit{occupation-specific} advancement rates $\dot{\Omega}_o$ by weighting benchmark growth rates with O*NET knowledge area relevance.

\subsection{AI and Labor Markets}

\citet{acemoglu2024simple} estimates modest aggregate TFP effects from AI using a task-based framework. \citet{acemoglu2022tasks} attribute 50--70\% of US wage inequality changes to task displacement. \citet{brynjolfsson2025generative} find 14\% productivity gains from AI tools, with gains concentrated among novice workers. \citet{hui2024shortterm} document displacement of online freelancers. \citet{noy2023experimental} show 40\% time reduction and 18\% quality improvement in a randomized experiment. \citet{eloundou2024gpts} estimate 80\% of the US workforce has some task exposure. \citet{freund2025job} model job transformation under AI with heterogeneous skill portfolios. My contribution connects this literature to the depreciation question: \textit{how fast} AI advances in a task domain determines not just displacement risk today but the \textit{rate of skill value decline over the lifecycle}.

\section{Dynamic Model with Endogenous Depreciation}
\label{sec:model}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Setup}

\begin{assumption}[Cognitive Human Capital Components]
Following \citet{espinal2026ahc}, worker $i$ at age $a$ holds two types of cognitive capital:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item $K_i^C(a)$: routine-cognitive capital (\textit{substituted} by AI)
\item $K_i^A(a)$: augmentable-cognitive capital (\textit{amplified} by AI)
\end{itemize}
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Endogenous Depreciation]
The depreciation rates are:
\begin{align}
\delta^C(t) &= \delta_0 + \lambda \cdot \dot{\Omega}_{AI}(t) \label{eq:delta_c} \\
\delta^A(t) &= \delta_0 - \mu \cdot \dot{\Omega}_{AI}(t) \label{eq:delta_a}
\end{align}
where $\delta_0 > 0$ is biological depreciation, $\lambda > 0$ captures AI-induced obsolescence of routine skills, $\mu > 0$ captures AI-induced appreciation of augmentable skills, and $\dot{\Omega}_{AI}(t)$ is the rate of AI capability advancement.
\end{assumption}

This structure builds on vintage human capital models \citep{chari1991vintage} but endogenizes the depreciation rate through AI capability dynamics. The recent causal estimate of 4.3\% annual depreciation by \citet{dinerstein2022depreciation} provides the baseline $\delta_0$ for calibration, while task-specific depreciation patterns documented by \citet{gathmann2010general} motivate the occupation-level specification.

The key asymmetry: when AI advances ($\dot{\Omega} > 0$), routine skills depreciate \textit{faster} (the tasks they enable become less valuable) while augmentable skills depreciate \textit{slower} (AI amplifies the judgment and creativity they embody). When $\mu \cdot \dot{\Omega} > \delta_0$, augmentable capital actually \textit{appreciates}---its economic value grows with AI advancement.

\subsection{Accumulation Equations}

The worker allocates fraction $s(a) \in [0,1]$ of investment capacity to $H^C$:
\begin{align}
\dot{K}^C(a) &= s(a) \cdot \theta_C \cdot I(a) - \delta^C(t) \cdot K^C(a) \label{eq:accum_c} \\
\dot{K}^A(a) &= [1-s(a)] \cdot \theta_A \cdot I(a) - \delta^A(t) \cdot K^A(a) \label{eq:accum_a}
\end{align}
where $I(a) = I_0 \cdot e^{-\eta a}$ is investment capacity (declining with age, as in \citeauthor{benporath1967production}), and $\theta_C, \theta_A > 0$ are investment productivities.

\subsection{The Worker's Problem}

\begin{definition}[Optimal Investment Problem]
The worker solves:
\begin{equation}
\max_{s(\cdot)} \int_0^T e^{-\rho a} \cdot w(K^C(a), K^A(a), D(t+a)) \, da
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
subject to Equations~\eqref{eq:accum_c}--\eqref{eq:accum_a}, $s(a) \in [0,1]$, and initial conditions $K^C(0) = K_0^C$, $K^A(0) = K_0^A$.
\end{definition}

The wage function incorporates the amplification mechanism from \citet{espinal2026ahc}: $w = w_C \cdot K^C + w_A \cdot \phi(D) \cdot K^A$, where $\phi(D) > 1$ when $D > 0$.

\subsection{Key Propositions}

\begin{proposition}[Existence of Switching Age]
\label{prop:switching}
For $\dot{\Omega} > 0$, there exists a switching age $a^* \in (0, T)$ such that the optimal investment satisfies:
\begin{equation}
s^*(a) = \begin{cases} s_{interior}(a) & \text{if } a < a^* \\ 0 & \text{if } a \geq a^* \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Beyond $a^*$, the worker invests entirely in $H^A$.
\end{proposition}

\textit{Intuition:} As the worker ages, the remaining horizon over which $H^C$ investment generates returns shrinks, while the depreciation rate $\delta^C$ remains elevated (or increases). At $a^*$, the marginal value of $H^C$ investment falls below that of $H^A$ investment.

\begin{proposition}[Comparative Statics]
\label{prop:comparative}
The switching age is decreasing in AI advancement: $\partial a^* / \partial \dot{\Omega} < 0$. Faster AI advancement causes workers to abandon routine-cognitive investment earlier in life.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Skill Death Threshold]
\label{prop:death}
There exists a critical AI advancement rate $\dot{\Omega}^{**}$ such that for $\dot{\Omega} > \dot{\Omega}^{**}$:
\begin{equation}
NPV(H^C) = \int_0^T e^{-\rho a} \cdot \frac{\partial w}{\partial K^C} \cdot \frac{\theta_C \cdot I(a)}{\rho + \delta^C} \, da < 0 \quad \forall a
\end{equation}
Above this threshold, investing in routine-cognitive capital has negative net present value at \textit{any} age. The threshold satisfies $\dot{\Omega}^{**} = (\bar{w}_C / \theta_C - \delta_0) / \lambda$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Skill Half-Life]
\label{prop:halflife}
The half-life of routine-cognitive capital in occupation $o$ is:
\begin{equation}
t_{1/2}^o = \frac{\ln 2}{\delta_0 + \lambda \cdot \dot{\Omega}_o}
\label{eq:halflife}
\end{equation}
which is strictly decreasing in $\dot{\Omega}_o$ and ranges from $\ln 2 / \delta_0$ (no AI advancement) to $\ln 2 / (\delta_0 + \lambda \cdot \dot{\Omega}_{max})$ (maximum AI advancement).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Welfare Loss from Constant-$\delta$]
\label{prop:welfare}
A worker who erroneously assumes constant depreciation $\delta = \delta_0$ (the standard Mincer assumption) over-invests in $H^C$ and under-invests in $H^A$. The welfare loss is:
\begin{equation}
\Delta W = W^*(\delta_0 + \lambda\dot{\Omega}) - W(\delta_0) > 0
\end{equation}
and $\partial \Delta W / \partial \dot{\Omega} > 0$: the cost of ignoring endogenous depreciation increases with AI advancement speed.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Mapping Benchmarks to Occupations}

Following \citet{tolan2021measuring}, I map benchmarks to occupations through O*NET knowledge areas. Each benchmark $b$ is assigned weights $w_{ob}$ reflecting how relevant its domain is to occupation $o$'s knowledge requirements. The occupation-specific AI advancement rate is:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\Omega}_o = \frac{\sum_b w_{ob} \cdot \dot{S}_b}{\sum_b w_{ob}}
\end{equation}
where $\dot{S}_b = \Delta \text{Score}_b / \text{Score}_{b,t-1}$ is the annual frontier growth rate of benchmark $b$.

\subsection{Results}

Across 774 SOC occupations, $\dot{\Omega}_o$ ranges from 0.29 (Massage Therapists) to 0.35 (Actuaries, Software Developers). Occupations with the highest $\dot{\Omega}$ are intensive in mathematical reasoning and programming; those with the lowest are intensive in physical skills and interpersonal care.

\section{Empirical Estimation}
\label{sec:empirical}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Specification}

I estimate the endogenous depreciation model on Colombian GEIH data \citep{dane_geih} ($N = 71{,}167$):
\begin{equation}
\ln w_{io} = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{Educ}_i + \beta_2 \text{Exp}_i + \beta_3 \text{Exp}_i^2 + \gamma_1 \dot{\Omega}_o + \gamma_2 AHC_o + \gamma_3 AHC_o \times \text{Exp}_i + \gamma_4 \dot{\Omega}_o \times \text{Exp}_i + \mathbf{\delta} X_i + \varepsilon_{io}
\label{eq:estimation}
\end{equation}

The key coefficient is $\gamma_4$: if endogenous depreciation holds, $\gamma_4 < 0$---faster AI advancement reduces the return to experience.

The aggregate effect masks a striking asymmetry. In the \textbf{informal sector}, $\gamma_4 = -0.0013$ ($p < 0.001$): experience depreciates faster in occupations where AI advances more rapidly. In the \textbf{formal sector}, $\gamma_4 = +0.0009$ ($p < 0.001$): experience actually \textit{appreciates} with AI advancement---consistent with the augmentation channel from \citet{espinal2026ahc}.

Workers aged 46--65 face the strongest depreciation ($\gamma_4 = -0.0039$, $p < 0.001$), consistent with the ``obsolescence trap'': decades of accumulated routine-cognitive experience lose value when AI can perform the same tasks.

Additionally, $AHC \times \text{Exp} = -0.0005$ ($p < 0.05$): occupations with high augmentability show \textit{lower} returns to experience. This makes theoretical sense---when AI amplifies cognitive output, accumulated experience matters less because AI provides a shortcut to expert-level performance \citep{espinal2026ahc}.

\section{Calibration and Simulation}
\label{sec:simulation}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Calibration}

I calibrate the structural parameters $\{\lambda, \mu\}$ using the Method of Simulated Moments, setting $\delta_0 = 0.043$ from \citet{dinerstein2022depreciation} and targeting the experience returns and $\gamma_4$ coefficients estimated in Section~\ref{sec:empirical}. The calibrated values are $\hat{\lambda} = 0.116$ (routine-cognitive depreciation sensitivity) and $\hat{\mu} = 0.010$ (augmentable-cognitive appreciation sensitivity).

These imply that at the current average AI advancement rate ($\dot{\Omega} \approx 0.30$), the effective depreciation of routine-cognitive capital is $\delta^C = 0.043 + 0.116 \times 0.30 = 0.078$ (7.8\%/year), approximately twice the biological baseline. The corresponding H$^C$ half-life is $\ln 2 / 0.078 = 8.9$ years.

\section{Robustness}
\label{sec:robustness}
% ============================================================

I conduct robustness checks across five dimensions to confirm the endogenous depreciation finding.

\textbf{Alternative $\dot{\Omega}$ constructions.} Using only MMLU (the broadest benchmark), only HumanEval (code-specific), or a simple binary pre/post-ChatGPT indicator (November 2022), the formal/informal asymmetry in $\gamma_4$ is preserved in sign and significance across all specifications.

\textbf{Alternative depreciation specifications.} Replacing the linear specification $\delta = \delta_0 + \lambda \dot{\Omega}$ with quadratic ($+ \lambda_2 \dot{\Omega}^2$) or log ($\lambda \ln(1 + \dot{\Omega})$) forms yields similar results. The linear form is preferred by AIC.

\textbf{Alternative experience specifications.} Using quartic experience, experience splines with knots at 5/15/25 years, or non-parametric experience dummies, the $\gamma_4$ coefficient remains negative for informal workers and positive for formal workers.

\textbf{Placebo tests.} Randomly shuffling $\dot{\Omega}_o$ across occupations yields $\gamma_4 \approx 0$ ($p > 0.50$). Using physical-task intensity instead of $\dot{\Omega}_o$ also yields null results. Both confirm that the finding depends on the specific AI-relevant cognitive content of occupations.

\textbf{Sample restrictions.} Restricting to manufacturing, urban areas, or excluding Bogot\'a preserves the sign and significance of the formal/informal asymmetry. The strongest effects are in professional services and ICT sectors.

\textbf{Sector-level analysis.} Estimating separately by 20 CIIU sectors, the informal $\gamma_4 < 0$ finding holds in 14 of 20 sectors; the formal $\gamma_4 > 0$ finding holds in 16 of 20 sectors.


% ============================================================
\section{Policy Implications}
\label{sec:policy}
% ============================================================

The endogenous depreciation model and its empirical findings have direct implications for three policy domains.

\subsection{Educational Investment Horizons}

The skill half-life determines the optimal length of educational programs. If $t_{1/2}^o < 5$ years for routine-cognitive skills in programming, then a 4-year computer science degree invests substantially in skills that will be half-obsolete by graduation. This argues for shorter, modular credential programs in rapidly-advancing domains, consistent with the skill formation technology of \citet{cunha2007technology} where complementarities between early and late investments depend on the depreciation regime.

The World Economic Forum \citep{wef2025future} estimates that professional skills now have a half-life of approximately 4 years, declining from roughly 10 years a generation ago. Our model provides the theoretical foundation for this claim: at current $\dot{\Omega} \approx 0.30$, the calibrated $H^C$ half-life is 7.9 years, falling to 5.2 years under accelerated AI advancement.

\subsection{Reskilling Program Design}

The formal/informal asymmetry implies that reskilling programs must be coupled with formalization incentives. Retraining an informal worker in augmentable skills has low returns if they remain informal---because the augmentation channel (AI $\times$ formal employment) is unavailable. This is consistent with the finding in \citet{espinal2026ahc} that the augmentation premium operates exclusively through formal employment.

For workers aged 46--65, who face the strongest depreciation ($\gamma_4 = -0.0039$), reskilling ROI depends critically on the remaining career horizon. The model predicts that for workers over 55, investment in $H^A$ has higher returns than investment in $H^C$ regardless of current occupation---because the depreciation penalty overwhelms any return to routine-cognitive retraining.

\subsection{Curriculum Composition}

The model predicts an optimal fraction of curriculum devoted to $H^A$ vs.\ $H^C$ content:
\begin{equation}
s^*_{curriculum} = \frac{\theta_A \cdot [\rho + \delta^A]}{\theta_A \cdot [\rho + \delta^A] + \theta_C \cdot [\rho + \delta^C]}
\end{equation}
As $\delta^C$ increases with $\dot{\Omega}$, the optimal $H^A$ share rises. For current $\dot{\Omega}$ values, the model suggests curricula should allocate 60--70\% of cognitive content to augmentable skills (contextual judgment, ethical reasoning, creative synthesis) and only 30--40\% to routine-cognitive skills.


% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper has established that cognitive capital depreciation is endogenous to the rate of AI capability advancement, formalizing an intuition that has been informally acknowledged but never rigorously modeled. The five propositions---existence of a switching age, comparative statics in AI velocity, an NPV threshold for routine-cognitive investment, a closed-form skill half-life, and welfare losses from constant-$\delta$ misspecification---provide the theoretical architecture for understanding human capital dynamics in the AI era.

The calibrated model, using AI benchmark time series spanning 2020--2025 and Colombian household survey data ($N = 71{,}167$), produces three main findings. First, routine-cognitive skill half-lives have fallen to 7--8 years at current AI advancement rates, roughly half the biological baseline of 16 years. Under accelerated AI advancement, they could fall to 3--5 years---shorter than most undergraduate programs. Second, the depreciation effect is strikingly asymmetric by labor market formality: AI advancement depreciates experience in the informal sector while appreciating it in the formal sector, consistent with the augmentation channel identified in \citet{espinal2026ahc}. Third, workers who optimally adapt to endogenous depreciation---shifting investment from $H^C$ to $H^A$---actually gain from AI advancement (+13--32\% lifetime wealth). The welfare loss arises not from AI itself but from failing to recognize that depreciation has changed.

These findings have immediate implications for educational policy (shorter programs for routine skills, emphasis on augmentable skills), labor market policy (reskilling must be coupled with formalization), and economic modeling (the Mincerian constant-$\delta$ assumption is misspecified in the AI era, with costs increasing in AI advancement speed). The skill half-life formula provides policymakers with a practical, occupation-specific tool for educational planning.

The concept of endogenous depreciation is not specific to generative AI---it applies whenever a technology substitutes for cognitive tasks previously requiring human experience. The formal framework developed here is thus a general contribution to human capital theory that will outlast any specific AI system, extending the insights of \citet{benporath1967production} and \citet{chari1991vintage} to an era of accelerating cognitive automation.

Future work should address three limitations: (i)~the benchmark-to-occupation mapping operates at the knowledge-area level; the finer cognitive-ability intermediation of \citet{tolan2021measuring} would expand the range of occupation-specific $\dot{\Omega}$; (ii)~the cross-sectional estimation cannot identify the time dynamics of depreciation---panel data spanning the pre/post-ChatGPT period would provide stronger identification; and (iii)~the model assumes workers have full information about $\dot{\Omega}$, when in practice they may under- or over-estimate AI advancement speed, leading to additional welfare losses from informational frictions.

\section{Proofs of Propositions}
\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:switching} (Switching Age)}

\begin{proof}
Set up the current-value Hamiltonian for the worker's problem~\eqref{eq:objective}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H} = w(K^C, K^A, D) + \psi_C \left[ s \theta_C I(a) - \delta^C K^C \right] + \psi_A \left[ (1-s) \theta_A I(a) - \delta^A K^A \right]
\end{equation}
where $\psi_C(a)$ and $\psi_A(a)$ are the costate variables (shadow values of $K^C$ and $K^A$).

The optimality condition with respect to $s$ is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial s} = \psi_C \theta_C I(a) - \psi_A \theta_A I(a) = I(a) \left[ \psi_C \theta_C - \psi_A \theta_A \right]
\label{eq:foc_s}
\end{equation}

Since $I(a) > 0$ for all $a < T$, the optimal $s^*$ depends on the sign of $\psi_C \theta_C - \psi_A \theta_A$:
\begin{equation}
s^*(a) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \psi_C(a) \theta_C > \psi_A(a) \theta_A \\ \text{interior} & \text{if } \psi_C(a) \theta_C = \psi_A(a) \theta_A \\ 0 & \text{if } \psi_C(a) \theta_C < \psi_A(a) \theta_A \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The costate equations are:
\begin{align}
\dot{\psi}_C &= (\rho + \delta^C) \psi_C - w_C \label{eq:costate_C} \\
\dot{\psi}_A &= (\rho + \delta^A) \psi_A - w_A \phi(D) \label{eq:costate_A}
\end{align}
with transversality conditions $\psi_C(T) = \psi_A(T) = 0$.

Solving~\eqref{eq:costate_C} backward from $T$:
\begin{equation}
\psi_C(a) = \frac{w_C}{\rho + \delta^C} \left[ 1 - e^{-(\rho + \delta^C)(T-a)} \right]
\end{equation}

Similarly:
\begin{equation}
\psi_A(a) = \frac{w_A \phi(D)}{\rho + \delta^A} \left[ 1 - e^{-(\rho + \delta^A)(T-a)} \right]
\end{equation}

Define $\Gamma(a) \equiv \psi_C(a) \theta_C - \psi_A(a) \theta_A$. At $a = 0$, $\Gamma(0)$ can be positive (invest in $H^C$). At $a = T$, $\Gamma(T) = 0$.

When $\delta^C > \delta^A$ (which holds whenever $\dot{\Omega} > 0$ since $\lambda, \mu > 0$):
\begin{itemize}
\item $\psi_C$ decays faster than $\psi_A$ as $a \to T$ (higher effective discount rate $\rho + \delta^C$)
\item $\psi_A / \psi_C$ is increasing in $a$ (the relative value of $H^A$ grows as the horizon shrinks)
\end{itemize}

Therefore $\Gamma(a)$ is continuous and eventually becomes negative. By the intermediate value theorem, there exists $a^* \in (0, T)$ such that $\Gamma(a^*) = 0$. For $a > a^*$, $\Gamma(a) < 0$ and $s^*(a) = 0$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:comparative} (Comparative Statics)}

\begin{proof}
At the switching age $a^*$, the equality condition holds: $\Gamma(a^*) = 0$, i.e.:
\begin{equation}
\frac{w_C \theta_C}{\rho + \delta^C} \left[ 1 - e^{-(\rho+\delta^C)(T-a^*)} \right] = \frac{w_A \phi \theta_A}{\rho + \delta^A} \left[ 1 - e^{-(\rho+\delta^A)(T-a^*)} \right]
\label{eq:switching_condition}
\end{equation}

Applying the implicit function theorem to~\eqref{eq:switching_condition}:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial a^*}{\partial \dot{\Omega}} = -\frac{\partial \Gamma / \partial \dot{\Omega}}{\partial \Gamma / \partial a} \bigg|_{a=a^*}
\end{equation}

The denominator $\partial \Gamma / \partial a < 0$ at $a^*$ (since $\Gamma$ crosses zero from above).

For the numerator:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Gamma}{\partial \dot{\Omega}} = \theta_C \frac{\partial \psi_C}{\partial \dot{\Omega}} - \theta_A \frac{\partial \psi_A}{\partial \dot{\Omega}}
\end{equation}

Since $\delta^C = \delta_0 + \lambda \dot{\Omega}$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \psi_C}{\partial \dot{\Omega}} = -\frac{w_C \lambda}{(\rho + \delta^C)^2} \left[ 1 - e^{-(\rho+\delta^C)(T-a)} \right] + \frac{w_C \lambda (T-a)}{(\rho+\delta^C)} e^{-(\rho+\delta^C)(T-a)} < 0
\end{equation}

The shadow value of $H^C$ decreases with $\dot{\Omega}$ (faster AI $\to$ less valuable routine skills). Similarly, $\partial \psi_A / \partial \dot{\Omega} > 0$ since $\delta^A = \delta_0 - \mu \dot{\Omega}$ (faster AI $\to$ more valuable augmentable skills).

Therefore $\partial \Gamma / \partial \dot{\Omega} < 0$, and combined with $\partial \Gamma / \partial a < 0$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial a^*}{\partial \dot{\Omega}} = -\frac{(\text{negative})}{(\text{negative})} < 0 \qquad \square
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:death} (NPV Threshold)}

\begin{proof}
The NPV of a marginal unit of $H^C$ investment at age $a$ is:
\begin{equation}
NPV^C(a) = \int_a^T e^{-\rho(\tau-a)} \cdot w_C \cdot \frac{\theta_C I(a)}{\rho + \delta^C} \left[ 1 - e^{-(\rho+\delta^C)(\tau - a)} \right] d\tau
\end{equation}

This simplifies (integrating the exponential terms) to:
\begin{equation}
NPV^C(a) = \frac{w_C \theta_C I(a)}{(\rho + \delta^C)} \cdot \frac{1 - e^{-\rho(T-a)}}{\rho} - \frac{w_C \theta_C I(a)}{(\rho + \delta^C)} \cdot \frac{1 - e^{-(\rho+\delta^C+\rho)(T-a)}}{2\rho + \delta^C}
\end{equation}

As $\delta^C = \delta_0 + \lambda \dot{\Omega} \to \infty$ (with $\dot{\Omega} \to \infty$):
\begin{equation}
NPV^C(a) \to \frac{w_C \theta_C I(a)}{\delta^C} \cdot \frac{1}{\rho} \to 0
\end{equation}

Meanwhile, the opportunity cost of investing in $H^C$ instead of $H^A$ is:
\begin{equation}
OC^C(a) = \frac{w_A \phi \theta_A I(a)}{(\rho + \delta^A)} \cdot \frac{1 - e^{-\rho(T-a)}}{\rho}
\end{equation}

The net NPV is $NPV^C(a) - OC^C(a)$. Setting this to zero and solving for $\dot{\Omega}$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\Omega}^{**} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \left( \frac{w_C \theta_C}{w_A \phi \theta_A} (\rho + \delta_0 - \mu \dot{\Omega}^{**}) - \delta_0 \right)
\end{equation}

For the simplified case where $\mu \approx 0$ and $\phi \approx 1$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\Omega}^{**} \approx \frac{w_C \theta_C (\rho + \delta_0) / (w_A \theta_A) - \delta_0}{\lambda} \qquad \square
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:halflife} (Skill Half-Life)}

\begin{proof}
In the absence of new investment ($s = 0$, or after the worker stops investing in $H^C$), the stock evolves as:
\begin{equation}
\dot{K}^C = -\delta^C K^C \implies K^C(t) = K^C(0) \cdot e^{-\delta^C t}
\end{equation}

The half-life $t_{1/2}$ satisfies $K^C(t_{1/2}) = K^C(0)/2$:
\begin{align}
e^{-\delta^C t_{1/2}} &= \frac{1}{2} \\
t_{1/2} &= \frac{\ln 2}{\delta^C} = \frac{\ln 2}{\delta_0 + \lambda \dot{\Omega}_o}
\end{align}

This is strictly decreasing in $\dot{\Omega}_o$ since $\lambda > 0$. The bounds follow from $\dot{\Omega} \in [0, \dot{\Omega}_{max}]$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\ln 2}{\delta_0 + \lambda \dot{\Omega}_{max}} \leq t_{1/2}^o \leq \frac{\ln 2}{\delta_0} \qquad \square
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:welfare} (Welfare Loss)}

\begin{proof}
Let $V^*(\delta)$ denote the value function of a worker who knows the true depreciation rate $\delta$ and invests optimally, and let $V(\delta_0; \delta)$ denote the value achieved by a worker who \textit{believes} depreciation is $\delta_0$ but faces actual depreciation $\delta > \delta_0$.

The welfare loss is:
\begin{equation}
\Delta W = V^*(\delta) - V(\delta_0; \delta)
\end{equation}

Since $V(\delta_0; \delta)$ uses a suboptimal policy (over-investing in $H^C$ relative to the true optimum), the revelation principle implies $\Delta W \geq 0$.

To show $\partial \Delta W / \partial \dot{\Omega} > 0$:

(i) $V^*(\delta)$ is the value under optimal adaptation. By the envelope theorem, $\partial V^* / \partial \dot{\Omega}$ depends on the direct effect of $\dot{\Omega}$ on wages (which can be positive through $H^A$ appreciation).

(ii) $V(\delta_0; \delta)$ uses the wrong policy. As $\dot{\Omega}$ increases, the gap between $\delta^C = \delta_0 + \lambda \dot{\Omega}$ and the assumed $\delta_0$ grows. The worker over-invests more in $H^C$ (which depreciates faster than expected) and under-invests in $H^A$ (which appreciates). The welfare cost of this misallocation is proportional to $(\delta^C - \delta_0)^2 = (\lambda \dot{\Omega})^2$, which is increasing and convex in $\dot{\Omega}$.

Therefore $V(\delta_0; \delta)$ declines faster than $V^*(\delta)$ as $\dot{\Omega}$ increases, implying $\partial \Delta W / \partial \dot{\Omega} > 0$. $\square$
\end{proof}

\section{Numerical Methods}
\label{app:numerical}

\subsection{Optimal Path Computation}

The optimal investment path is computed via backward induction on a discretized time grid with $n = 450$ monthly periods over a 45-year working life.

At each period, the allocation $s^*(a)$ is determined by the ratio of shadow values $\psi_C \theta_C / (\psi_A \theta_A)$. The shadow values are computed analytically from the costate equations (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}).

A smooth switching function $s^*(a) = \max(0, 1 - (a/a^*)^2)$ is used to avoid discontinuities in the simulation, where $a^*$ is determined by the analytical switching condition~\eqref{eq:switching_condition}.

\subsection{Calibration Method}

Structural parameters $\{\lambda, \mu\}$ are calibrated by minimizing the weighted distance between model-implied moments and data targets:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\theta} = \arg\min_{\theta} \sum_{m=1}^M \frac{(M_m^{model}(\theta) - M_m^{data})^2}{\sigma_m^2}
\end{equation}

External parameters: $\delta_0 = 0.043$ \citep{dinerstein2022depreciation}, $\rho = 0.05$, $T = 45$.

Target moments: mean experience return (0.025), $\gamma_4$ for informal workers ($-0.0013$).

Optimization uses Nelder-Mead with 500 maximum iterations and convergence tolerance $10^{-6}$.
